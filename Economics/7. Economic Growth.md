> [!def] 
> the increase in the level of national output, measured by annual percentage change in GDP

## Gross Domestic Product
> [!def] **Money/Nominal GDP**
> the *monetary* *value* of **finished** goods and services produced within a country for a given period of time

> national output = national income = national expenditure

$$GDP = C+I+G+(X-M)$$
Consumption: Household spending on consumer goods
Investment: Business spending on capital goods
Government spending: Total consumption and investment expenditure of the government, **Government Purchasing**
Exports - Imports

**Real GDP**: GDP adjusted for inflation
**GDP per head/capita**: key measure of a country's economic growth and standards of living, GDP/population

> As prices rise, to total value of output(*nominal GDP*) will also rise, but there is no actual increase in the volume of goods and services produced.
> If the volume of goods and services expands, the *real GDP* increases.
## Consumer Goods
> products used by consumers

## Capital Goods
> goods used to produce other goods

## Benefits
**Individual**: employment opportunities, rising income had higher living standard
**Firms**: rising sales and profits, more investment in capital equipment
**Government**: higher tax revenue, improving public service
**The economy**: low unemployment

## Costs
**Individual**: exploitation of labour and reduce quality of life
**Firms**: rising production cost
**The economy**:
- Environmental consequences
- Risk of inflation (especially demand-side driven)
- Inequalities in income and wealth
- Resource depletion

## Recession
### Causes
- Fall in aggregate demand, which reduces economic activity
	- Fall in consumption
	- Fall in investment
	- Fall in government spending
	- Fall in net exports
- fall in aggregate supply, which reduces the quality or quantity of resources in an economy
	- depletion of natural resources
	- natural disasters
	- net outflow of skilled workers from the country
	- rising costs of production
