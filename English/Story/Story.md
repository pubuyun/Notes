Dusk settled over the abandoned station as Liam stood alone on the cracked platform, rain soaking through his trainers. The rails stretched into the mist, rusted and faintly gleaming beneath the last smear of light. Broken tiles lay scattered at his feet, and the timetable on the wall hung faded and peeling, its ink almost erased by ten years of weather. The air smelled of wet iron and rotting wood.

For a moment, he imagined he heard his grandfather’s pocket watch ticking, steady and precise, followed by the distant sigh of a train pulling in. He could almost feel that familiar hand on his shoulder.

But when he opened his eyes, there was only the rain—cold against his face—and the hollow silence of a station that no longer remembered him.

---
“Excuse me,” the girl asked, her voice cutting gently through the rain, “when is the next train to London?”

Liam turned, startled. She stood a few steps away, drenched, her hair clinging to her pale face. “There aren’t any trains,” he said. “This station closed years ago.” His tone was calm, almost rehearsed, as though he had explained this before—to himself.

She shook her head. “I’m meeting someone. I have to wait.”

He studied her for a moment. There was determination in her posture, but her eyes seemed strangely distant. Sighing, he slipped off his jacket and placed it over her shoulders. “You’ll freeze,” he muttered.

When she asked why he had come, Liam glanced toward the dead lamp at the end of the platform. “My grandfather used to light that,” he said softly. “He said it helped people find their way home.”

---
Night gathered fully around them, pressing close as the mist thickened. Liam was about to insist they leave when the dead lamp at the end of the platform flickered.

Then—

It lit.

A thin spark trembled within the rusted glass before swelling into a steady, amber glow. The light spread outward, staining the fog with warmth, turning each drifting raindrop into a thread of gold. The platform, moments ago hollow and colourless, seemed to breathe again under its glow. Liam felt the air shift, charged and trembling, as if time itself had paused to watch.

From somewhere beyond the darkened tracks came the long, aching call of a steam whistle.

Beside him, the girl smiled—bright, relieved. “He’s here,” she whispered.

She stepped forward into the light, and as the amber glow wrapped around her, her outline softened, fading like mist beneath the rising sun.

---
Liam’s breath caught in his throat. The realisation came slowly, not as shock but as understanding. She had never been lost. She had been waiting.

He turned towards the lamp, its amber glow steady now, almost protective. For a fleeting second, he thought he saw a figure standing within the light—broad shoulders, cap tilted slightly forward, a familiar stillness. His grandfather had always said the lamp was more than metal and wire; it was a promise that no one would be left in the dark.

When Liam looked back, the platform was empty.

She was gone.

Only his jacket remained, neatly folded on the bench where she had sat. The whistle sounded once more, softer this time, fading into the distance like a final farewell.

---
The rain stopped as quietly as it had begun. Mist loosened its grip on the platform, drifting away to reveal the tracks lying silver beneath the lamp’s steady glow. The air felt different now—cleaner, lighter—carrying the faint scent of wet earth instead of rust.

Liam walked slowly towards the lamp. He placed his hand against the cold metal pole, half expecting it to feel ordinary. It did not. The warmth from the light above settled over him, not on his skin but somewhere deeper, steadying him. The ache in his chest had thinned into something softer, something bearable.

He picked up his jacket and slipped it on. After one last look at the illuminated platform, he turned away. His shadow stretched long behind him, held briefly in the amber light.

He went home.
