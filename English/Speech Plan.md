## Full speech
### Start
Good morning everyone,

One day, my parent suddenly came over and said, “You’re falling way behind everyone else—go study immediately!”

Well, it all started when a while ago, my parent posted a photo of me at a school event on WeChat. That’s it. Just one innocent post. And suddenly, their feed was full of videos and posts about other kids— with perfect grades, amazing talents, awards, competitions. 

That wasn’t an accident—the algorithm had decided what my parent should see, and it fed them content designed to make comparisons stronger and emotions sharper. That’s the danger: it doesn’t just show us content, it shapes what we think and feel.

So here’s my question: Who’s in control—you, or the algorithm
### Mental Health
Algorithms are supposed to keep us entertained, right? But sometimes they cross a dangerous line. They don’t just recommend harmless dance videos or cat memes—they notice when someone feels vulnerable. The algorithm recognizes vulnerability and, instead of seeing it as something it should be careful around, it sees it as a potential point of addiction

One researcher ran an experiment: change a TikTok account’s name from “Sarah” to “Sarah Lose Weight.” Instantly, the algorithm started sending that account **12 times more self-harm content**. Imagine how dangerous that is.

Another study found that after five or six hours of scrolling, nearly **one in every two videos** shown to certain accounts was about mental health, often harmful or misleading. For people already struggling, that means more stress, more lost sleep, more depression.

The algorithm doesn’t ask: “Is this good for you?” It only asks: “Will this keep you watching?”

### Misinformation
But it’s not just about mental health. Algorithms also shape what we believe is true.

Their design is simple: maximize watch time. Show the right video to the right person at the right moment. 

During the pandemic, we saw the cost of that. Fake news about miracle cures or conspiracy theories spread faster than verified facts—because lies, apparently, are more entertaining than truth.

The result? Echo chambers. People only hear voices that agree with them. Views become extreme. And eventually, trust in reliable information—whether science, government, or media—breaks down.

All because the algorithm wants you to keep scrolling.

### Privacy

And let’s not forget privacy.

Over a million underage children were exposed to harmful content because platforms collected their personal data without protection. That data—your clicks, your likes, your face, even your voice—is tracked and profiled.

That’s why so many of us have had the same creepy experience: you talk about a product with your friends, and suddenly an ad for it pops up on Instagram. It feels like someone’s listening in.

But it’s not just ads. Your preferences, your habits, even your fears can be sold to advertisers, politicians, or anyone willing to pay. That means the algorithm doesn’t just know what you watch—it can predict what you’ll want, and maybe even push you toward it.

If strangers know your fears, insecurities, or weaknesses, they can use them against you. 
Scammers use leaked data to trick people. 

Imagine an elderly person who often searched for heart medicine online. A scammer, knowing this fear, offered a fake ‘cure.’ Because the scam spoke directly to their weakness, the victim trusted it and lost money, or even life—their private worry became a weapon.

### Taking Control

So what can we do? Throw away our phones? Lock ourselves out of TikTok?

Not necessarily. The solution isn’t to escape, but to take control.

Remember the purpose of the algorithm: not to inform you, not to heal you, but to keep you engaged. So the next time you’re scrolling:
- **Pause before trusting content.** Pause and ask yourself, “Is this credible, or just clickbait?”
- **Pause before treating posts as authority.** Pause and remember that not every “doctor” on YouTube is real.
- **Pause before following accounts that drain you.** Pause and choose accounts that inspire, educate, and support you.

The feed doesn’t have to feed on you—you can decide what you feed it.

And if we all start making those small choices, the power shifts. The algorithm loses its grip. We take back our time, our attention, and our freedom.

So here’s my challenge to you:
**Next time you scroll—pause. Next time you scroll—choose. Next time you scroll—decide who’s in control.**

Thank you.

### **Q1. If algorithms are harmful, why don’t social media companies just fix them?**

**A:**  
Because their business model depends on attention. The longer you stay on the app, the more ads you see — and the more money they make. 
Fixing the algorithm to prioritize truth or mental health over engagement would _reduce profit_.  

Some companies have added “digital wellbeing” features, but those are often surface-level. 
Real change requires **transparency and regulation**, not just optional screen time reminders. 
Until then, users need to be aware of how the system works — and act intentionally inside it.

---

### **Q2. Are all algorithms bad? Can they ever help our wellbeing?**

**A:**  
Not all algorithms are harmful. Algorithms can be tools for good — for example, recommending educational videos, connecting people with support communities, or detecting hate speech.  
The difference lies in **purpose and design**.  
If an algorithm is trained to maximize _value_ (learning, empathy, creativity), it helps society.  
If it’s trained to maximize _engagement_ (clicks, time, emotion), it often harms.  
So, the goal isn’t to reject algorithms — it’s to demand **ethical algorithms** that serve people, not profit.

---
### **Q3. If we can take control ourselves, what can others — like governments, creators, and parents — do to help?**

**A:**  
Everyone in the system has power
- **Governments** can require **algorithmic transparency** — forcing companies to explain how recommendations are made, and to prove that their systems don’t exploit mental health or spread misinformation. They can also limit data collection and protect children’s online privacy.  
- **Creators** can choose to post responsibly, checking facts before sharing and avoiding harmful trends that exploit fear or insecurity.
- **Parents and peers** can start honest conversations about what we see online, breaking the illusion that everyone’s life is perfect.
When each group takes responsibility, the system shifts — not because algorithms suddenly become kind, but because **people around us choose to be conscious and ethical.**
