
The reading and the lecture are both about the extinction of Steller's sea cow. The author of the reading proposes three possible theories for the main cause. However, the lecturer casts doubt on the claims made in the article. He believes that there are problems with each of these theories.

Firstly, the author suggests that overhunting by native Siberian people may have caused the extinction. It’s mentioned that sea cows were a good food source and years of hunting could have reduced their numbers. The lecturer challenges this opinion. He claims that the sea cows were massive, for 9 meters long and 10 tons weight, so just a few could feed a village for months. Additionally, he believes that the native population was small and thus unlikely to hunt enough sea cows to cause extinction.

Secondly, the writer points out that ecosystem disturbances might have reduced the kelp, the sea cows' main food. The article notes that ecological changes before 1768 could have led to a kelp decrease. However, the lecturer rebuts this claim by saying that a major ecological event would have affected other marine animals, like whales. He suggests that since fishing ships reported no decline in whales, there was probably no major ecosystem problem. So that the kelp was likely growing well and the sea cows did not experience food shortage.

Lastly, it is stated in the article that European fur traders could be the main cause. The author mentions that the traders caught the last sea cow in 1768 and had efficient weapons. On the other hand, the lecturer argues that the sea cow population was already very small when the Europeans arrived. Furthermore, he explains that this is because evidence shows the population was largest hundreds of years before the 1700s, meaning something else was causing a serious and ongoing decline long before European traders came.
