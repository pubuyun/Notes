
TPO 35

The reading and the lecture are both about the origin of the Voynich manuscript. The author of the reading thinks there are three possible theories: it was a genuine scientific work by Anthony Ascham, a fake magical book by Edward Kelley, or a modern forgery by Wilfrid Voynich. However, the lecturer casts doubt on the claims made in the article. He believes that none of these individuals was likely the author.

Firstly, the author suggests that the manuscript could be a genuine scientific work written in a secret code by Anthony Ascham. It’s mentioned that many plant illustrations in the manuscript resemble those in Ascham’s book. The lecturer challenges this opinion. He claims that Ascham was an ordinary scientist whose books contained no original ideas. Additionally, he believes that it is unlikely Ascham would create such an elaborately coded document since his known works were based on common knowledge.

Secondly, the writer points out that the manuscript might be a fake created by Edward Kelley to sell to wealthy nobles. The article notes that Kelley used a made-up alphabet to produce a meaningless text. However, the lecturer rebuts this claim by saying that a complex manuscript like the Voynich would have been unnecessary for Kelley’s purpose. He suggests that people in the sixteenth century were easy to fool, so a much simpler fake would have sufficed. He also notes that there is no reason Kelley would have put so much effort into creating such a detailed forgery.

Lastly, it is stated in the article that the manuscript could be a modern fake created by Wilfrid Voynich himself. The author mentions that Voynich had the knowledge to create an authentic-looking old manuscript. On the other hand, the lecturer argues that scientific testing shows both the vellum and the ink are over 400 years old. Furthermore, he explains that this is because Voynich could not have obtained 400-year-old ink, proving the manuscript was created long before he acquired it.
