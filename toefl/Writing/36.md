The reading and the lecture are both about the effectiveness of cloud seeding in reducing hail damage to crops. The author of the reading thinks that cloud seeding is an effective method, supported by laboratory experiments, evidence from Asia, and local studies. However, the lecturer casts doubt on the claims made in the article. She believes that cloud seeding is not clearly effective and challenges each piece of evidence.

Firstly, the author suggests that laboratory experiments prove cloud seeding works. It’s mentioned that adding silver iodide to cold water vapor in the lab resulted in light snow instead of hail pellets. The lecturer challenges this opinion. She claims that in real-life conditions, silver iodide can prevent all precipitation, including snow and rain. Additionally, she believes that this could cause droughts in areas with little rain, damaging crops due to a lack of water instead of hail.

Secondly, the writer points out that evidence from Asia supports the effectiveness of cloud seeding. The article notes that some Asian countries have successfully used cloud seeding to control precipitation in urban areas. However, the lecturer rebuts this claim by saying that the success in Asia may not be replicable in the United States. She suggests that high pollution levels in Asian cities create favorable conditions for cloud seeding by interacting with the chemicals. She also notes that unpolluted farming regions in the U.S. may lack these conditions, making cloud seeding less effective there.

Lastly, it is stated in the article that a local study in the central U.S. supports the value of cloud seeding. The author mentions that this study found reduced hail damage in an area where cloud seeding was used. On the other hand, the lecturer argues that the study is not convincing because the reduction in hail damage occurred across the entire region, not just where cloud seeding was applied. Furthermore, she explains that this is because the decrease was likely due to natural weather variations that year, rather than the cloud seeding program.
